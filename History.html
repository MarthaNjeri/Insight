    Our History

    Founding and Early Years (1962-1963)

    The Limuru Dairy Farmers Co-operative Society was officially established on April 28, 1962, under the Co-operative Act. Operations commenced in March 1963 with a modest group of seven members in the central highlands of Kenya, specifically in Limuru sub-county, Kiambu Countyâ€”a region renowned for its exceptional milk quality. Membership grew steadily, reaching 76 members with the establishment of its first collection center, now known as Route 1.

    Expansion and Support (1963-1964)

    In 1963, UNICEF provided additional milk cans to support the fledgling cooperative. By 1964, recognizing the high costs farmers faced from private vendors, the management opened a store to supply farm inputs, feeds, and milk cans at more affordable rates, enhancing member benefits and operational efficiency.

    Land and Infrastructure (1967)

    The year 1967 marked significant progress as the society acquired its own land and purchased its first vehicle, bolstering its independence and logistical capabilities.

    Head Office Development (1984-1986)

    Construction of the societyâ€™s head office, a four-story building named Maziwa House, began in 1984 and was completed in November 1986, providing a permanent base for administrative and operational activities.

    Service Enhancements (1987)

    In 1987, with member approval and government backing, the society introduced Artificial Insemination and Clinical services, improving livestock health and productivity for its members.

    Milk Processing Begins (1997)

    By 1997, Limuru Milk Processor launched milk processing operations under the Limuru Fresh brand, marking a pivotal shift toward value-added dairy production.

    Modern Operations and Growth

    Today, the society boasts over 3,000 active milk suppliers and operates 37 milk collection centers, handling approximately 30,000 liters of milk daily. Owned entirely by smallholder farmers with equal shares and rights, the cooperative uses digital systems to collect, test, and bulk milk in strategically placed coolers. The raw milk is transported to the factory, where it undergoes quality checks, pasteurization, and packaging into 50 kg aluminum cans for distribution to hospitals, schools, supermarkets, and milk bars across Nairobi and surrounding areas.
